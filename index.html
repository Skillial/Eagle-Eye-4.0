Abstract

Modern smartphones now offer computational power comparable to desktop systems, enabling a wide range of mobile applications and making in-device photography increasingly widespread. With over six billion active users worldwide, capturing and sharing high-quality images has become central to digital life. However, mobile cameras still face challenges such as limited resolution, haze, shadows, and noise, which can compromise image clarity and visibility. To address these limitations, we developed Eagle Eye 4.0, a mobile camera application that integrates enhancement techniques including super-resolution, dehazing, shadow removal, and denoising, while utilizing design principles from existing camera applications. We evaluated the performance of the ported algorithms by focusing on image quality and verifying that the application worked reliably across a range of devices, including low-end, mid-range, and high-end smartphones. The results demonstrate that the algorithms were successfully adapted for mobile devices with minimal loss in performance. User feedback further demonstrates that the application is well received and suitable for use across a broad range of smartphones.

Application

Eagle Eye 4.0 is an Android-based mobile camera app developed in Kotlin with C++ components for image enhancement. It integrates super-resolution, dehazing, shadow removal, and denoising, designed for mobile use. The app features a familiar camera interface for immediate photo capture and customizable enhancement application. Its UI is designed for simplicity, offering a live preview, a settings page for technique management and camera controls, and an image gallery for viewing and managing enhanced photos.

Pipeline

Eagle Eye 4.0 incorporates four image enhancement techniques: super-resolution, dehazing, shadow removal, and denoising. All of these are adapted for mobile devices with limited resources. These adaptations include converting models to lightweight formats like ONNX and applying image reduction methods. The enhancement process follows a standardized five-stage pipeline: Capture acquire input images, Reduce limit pixels for memory or computational efficiency via techniques such as patch processing or downsampling, Process apply algorithms suited for mobile execution, Post-process enhance visual quality by recovering fine details and reducing artifacts, and Save store output images in widely supported formats.

The image enhancement techniques implemented in Eagle Eye 4.0 were evaluated based on both visual quality and computational performance. Visual quality was assessed using a combination of full-reference and perceptual metrics, including SSIM, PSNR, LPIPS, and DISTS. SSIM was calculated both against the output of the original implementation and against ground truth images, while PSNR was used to assess signal fidelity relative to the ground truth. In addition, LPIPS and DISTS were employed to evaluate perceptual quality, as these metrics are sensitive to human perception of texture, structure, and overall visual appearance. All evaluations were performed using a set of datasets carefully chosen for their relevance to different image enhancement tasks.
